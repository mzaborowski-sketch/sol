01
What is the Hub?

A permanent cooperation platform

The Hub is not a series of isolated meetings. It is intended to provide a stable place for working on specific energy-efficiency financing problems.

The Polish Hub brings together public administration, financial institutions, companies, local authorities, industry organisations, energy service companies, auditors and experts. Its purpose is not merely to exchange views, but to deliver results useful to organisations designing, implementing and financing investment.

Each topic should begin with a diagnosis, include verification of stakeholder positions and implementation conditions, and lead to recommendations or clearly described options for further action.

Connects perspectives

It brings together investor needs, public requirements, banking practice and technical expertise.

Works on specific barriers

It focuses on problems that can be described, verified and translated into implementable measures.

Produces usable results

It develops recommendations, standards, proposed changes, risk-allocation models and pilot solutions.

02
Why is it needed?

Capital is only part of the problem

Investment scale also depends on procedures, information quality, project-development costs and the way risk is allocated.

Public funds can cover only part of investment needs. It is therefore essential to mobilise private capital more effectively and design instruments and procedures that are understandable, proportionate and attractive to both investors and financial institutions.

The Hub will help identify where the system loses effectiveness: duplicated procedures, high transaction costs, insufficiently prepared projects and information on energy effects that cannot easily be used by financiers.

1

More private capital

Creating conditions in which private finance can complement and multiply public resources.

2

Better public leverage

Using grants, guarantees and blended instruments to mobilise additional finance.

3

Easier access to finance

Reducing duplicated requirements and matching procedures to project scale.

4

Lower risk and transaction costs

Better data, standardisation and appropriate allocation of responsibility.

5

Developing products and support models that provide a clear benefit to investors.

6

More investment-ready projects

Stimulating demand and supporting the development and aggregation of project pipelines.

03
Working method

From problem to recommendation

Working groups follow a staged process based on diagnosis and the feasibility of proposed solutions.

Diagnosis

Review of experience, barriers, stakeholder positions and questions requiring resolution.

Verification

Completing the evidence base and identifying agreement, divergence and missing information.

Solution design

Defining implementation conditions, options, responsibilities and possible pilots.

Recommendations

Assessing feasibility and preparing results that can be used in practice.

04
Initial topics

Two complementary working groups

The first area concerns the design of financial instruments; the second concerns the system conditions that determine whether those instruments can work effectively.

I

Development of repayable and blended instruments

The group will examine why loans, credit products and instruments combining repayable finance with public support have not always achieved the expected scale, and what needs to change to make them attractive to clients and banks.

Key question: how can the investor receive a clear benefit while the financial institution has a sound case for the cost of implementing and distributing the product?
II

Effectiveness of the financing system

The group will address non-financial system elements: procedures, information quality, methods for assessing energy effects, standardisation and the allocation of responsibility and risk.

Key question: how can credible information on energy effects be preserved while unnecessary analytical and procedural burdens are reduced?
